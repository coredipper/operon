\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We tested three biologically-grounded agent reliability features
against naive non-biological alternatives, with 10M+ data points
across 10 independent seeds.  Two features demonstrated clear
empirical benefits; one did not.

Metabolic priority gating provides a structural guarantee that
critical operations are served under resource pressure (100\% vs
39.8\% for a flat counter under bursty load).  Autoinducer-based
quorum sensing achieves zero false-positive rate with meaningful
true-positive rate (71--87\% at 40\% compromise), occupying a
precision--recall position unreachable by majority voting or
independent detection.  Bayesian two-signal stagnation detection
does not outperform cosine-similarity thresholds with mock embeddings,
though the result may change with real embedding models.

The consistent pattern: biological design earns complexity through
\emph{mechanism-level structural guarantees}---hard properties enforced
by design (priority gating, signal decay)---rather than through
algorithmic sophistication (Bayesian estimation, optimization).
This finding should guide the adoption of biological motifs in agent
systems: invest in structural mechanism design; be skeptical of
biological metaphors that promise better information processing.

\paragraph{Open problems.}
Real embedding models for the epiplexity benchmark.  Auto-calibration
of quorum sensing thresholds from population and signal statistics.
Empirical validation of epistemic topology bounds against measured
performance.  End-to-end evaluation with actual LLM agents performing
real tasks.
